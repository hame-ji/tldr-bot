**1) Title**
How to Instantly Be Better at Things 

**2) 4-6 Key Points**
*   **Role-based performance gains:** Asking yourself how a highly skilled person would approach a task can yield immediate improvements, a mechanism one reader compared to assigning an "expert persona" prompt to a Large Language Model [1, 2].
*   **Intuition over explicit reasoning:** Humans are natural "mimicry machines," but often underutilize this trait in favor of explicit reasoning and step-by-step instructions, which can actually degrade performance [3, 4]. 
*   **Holistic observation:** Emulating the "wholeness" of a successful process (like observing a tennis swing) is often more effective than trying to assemble a skill from explicit, component parts [4].
*   **General competence transfers:** Mimicking someone who possesses general capability and confidence can improve your execution, even if that person is not a domain expert in the specific task at hand [5].
*   **The "pre-competitive" nature of skills:** Comparative metrics (benchmarking against peers) are often arbitrary because many fields have not yet reached their true performance ceiling [6-8]. Once a new standard is proven achievable, others quickly adapt and surpass it [6, 7].

**3) Why it matters**
For a software engineer, this framework suggests that reading explicit documentation or relying purely on theoretical knowledge may sometimes be less effective than directly observing and mimicking the workflows, habits, and problem-solving approaches of senior developers [3, 4]. Furthermore, it highlights the danger of simply benchmarking your velocity or code quality against your immediate team [6]. Treating your current engineering skills as "pre-competitive" allows you to bypass arbitrary peer standards and aim for a much higher ceiling of excellence [7, 8].
