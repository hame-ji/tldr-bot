## Item 2

URL: https://youtu.be/l8pQeVVaqpY?si=o--UUEFxPoZUHkv3

1) Open source is dying
2) Key points:
    *   Open source projects, particularly critical infrastructure, are often maintained by individuals or small teams with insufficient funding.
    *   Large corporations frequently use open-source software extensively without proportionally contributing back financially or with development resources.
    *   This lack of reciprocal contribution leads to maintainer burnout and an unsustainable model for many projects.
    *   Some companies, whose business models rely heavily on open-source projects, have begun to adopt more restrictive "source available" licenses (e.g., SSPL, BSL) to protect their revenue and prevent cloud providers from offering their software as a service without contributing upstream.
    *   The term "open source" is increasingly being redefined by companies to suit their commercial interests, potentially diluting its original spirit of freedom and community.
3) Why it matters:
    *   Software engineers may encounter fewer truly permissively licensed open-source tools and libraries as projects move to more restrictive models.
    *   Understanding licensing changes (e.g., from MIT/Apache to SSPL/BSL) becomes crucial for project selection and compliance.
    *   The long-term viability and maintenance of critical open-source components could be at risk, impacting the stability and security of software built upon them.
    *   It highlights the importance of contributing back to open-source projects, both individually and corporately, to foster a sustainable ecosystem.
